🏆 English Department Award 2023

The Uncanny Familiar: Gothic Domesticity in Flannery O'Connor's Short Fiction

Academic Essay  ·  English Department, Mount St. Mary's University

This essay examines the domestic space as a site of Gothic disruption in O'Connor's fiction. It argues that the familiar household becomes the primary vehicle for her theological vision of grace and violence, drawing on Freud's concept of the uncanny and recent scholarship in American Gothic studies. Close readings of "A Good Man Is Hard to Find," "Good Country People," and "Everything That Rises Must Converge" demonstrate how O'Connor uses the home as a stage for the collision of the sacred and the grotesque.

Designing for Belonging: Universal Design for Learning and First-Generation Students

Academic Essay  ·  MEd ID&T Program

This essay examines how UDL principles can be applied to support first-generation college students in higher education, arguing that equitable design is not an accommodation but a structural commitment. It reviews current research on first-generation student experiences and proposes a framework for applying UDL at the course and program level.

Digital Humanities 2023

Haunted Archives: Folklore, Memory, and Digital Preservation

Conference Paper  ·  Digital Humanities Seminar

This paper considers the tension between digital preservation and the living, embodied nature of folklore. It asks what is lost when oral traditions and community memory are translated into database entries, and what responsibilities archivists and digital humanists have toward the communities whose stories they preserve.

Faulkner's Dust: Time, Decay, and the Southern Gothic Imagination

Academic Essay  ·  American Literature

This essay reads Faulkner's use of physical decay and material ruin as a formal strategy for representing the psychological and historical weight of the American South. It focuses on "A Rose for Emily" and "The Sound and the Fury" as texts in which the rotting house becomes a figure for the rotting mind.
